# Expanded Lesson Copy Companion: Learn Hong Kong Mahjong

Use this as a copy companion to the implementation PRD. Each lesson includes teacher-style explanatory copy, the main learning point, and notes for feedback/UI. The goal is to make each page feel like an in-person instructor is guiding the learner, not like a rulebook.

## Global lesson voice

Use short, confident explanations. Introduce one idea at a time. After each interactive check, explain why the answer is right or wrong in practical table language. Favor phrases like “At a real table…” and “The habit to build is…” so learners feel prepared to play.

## Lesson 1.1 Welcome to the Game

**Route:** `/learn/what-is-hong-kong-mahjong/welcome-to-the-game`

### Main teaching copy

Mahjong is a four-player tile game. Think of it as a race, but not a race where everyone runs in a straight line. Each player is quietly building a complete hand while also watching what the other players throw away. On your turn, you usually draw one tile, decide what your hand is trying to become, and discard one tile you no longer need. Sometimes another player’s discard is exactly what you need, so you call it and use it immediately. The hand ends when someone completes a legal winning hand, or when the wall runs out.

### Teacher-led emphasis

A beginner should leave this page understanding the whole game at a high level: four players, draw, discard, call, complete a hand.

### Suggested feedback language

- **Correct:** “Exactly. That is the rule you would use at the table.”
- **Almost:** “Close — check the suit, timing, or player position again.”
- **Incorrect:** “Not quite. Slow down and identify the tile group or table action before deciding.”

## Lesson 1.2 HK Mahjong vs Other Styles

**Route:** `/learn/what-is-hong-kong-mahjong/hk-mahjong-vs-other-styles`

### Main teaching copy

Mahjong is not one single universal game. It is a family of related games, like poker has Texas Hold’em, Omaha, and other variants. Hong Kong Mahjong is one of the most common Chinese-style rulesets. It is relatively fast, social, and call-friendly. This course teaches 13-tile Classical Hong Kong Mahjong, so when learners see rules from Japanese Riichi, Taiwanese 16-tile mahjong, American Mahjong, or other regional versions, they should expect differences.

## Lesson 1.3 The Objective of a Hand

**Route:** `/learn/what-is-hong-kong-mahjong/objective-of-a-hand`

### Main teaching copy

Your job in each hand is to be the first player to make a legal winning hand. The standard winning hand is four completed groups, called melds, plus one pair. A meld can be a sequence, a triplet, or a declared kong. The pair is two matching tiles. When you win, you will normally reveal your hand and show how the tiles break into those four melds and one pair. Shape comes first: before scoring or strategy matters, your tiles must actually form a legal hand.

## Lesson 1.5 How a Hand Flows

**Route:** `/learn/what-is-hong-kong-mahjong/how-a-hand-flows`

### Main teaching copy

A hand follows a predictable rhythm. First, players shuffle and build the wall. Then the wall is opened and tiles are dealt. East starts. Players take turns drawing and discarding. After each discard, other players get a chance to call that tile if it helps them. Eventually, someone wins, or the wall runs out and the hand is drawn. If someone wins, the table verifies the hand and scores it before moving on.

**Route:** `/learn/tiles-melds-winning-hands/the-three-suits`

### Main teaching copy

The three suits are Dots, Bamboos, and Characters. Each suit runs from 1 through 9, and there are four copies of each tile. A 3 Bamboo is different from a 3 Dot, even though both have the number 3. ## Lesson 2.4 Tile Groupings

**Route:** `/learn/tiles-melds-winning-hands/tile-groupings`

### Main teaching copy

A kong is four identical tiles declared as a kong. Some groups look close but are not valid: 2 Dot, 3 Bamboo, 4 Character is not a sequence because the suits do not match. ## Lesson 3.5 Live Wall vs Dead Wall

**Route:** `/learn/setup-and-dealing/live-wall-vs-dead-wall`

### Main teaching copy

On a normal turn, draw from the live wall. After a valid kong is declared and exposed, draw the supplement tile from the dead wall.

## Lesson 5.2 Chow

**Route:** `/learn/calls-chow-pung-kong-win/chow`

### Main teaching copy

Chow means using the latest discard to complete a three-tile sequence. You can Chow only from the player on your left. The sequence must be three consecutive numbers in the same suit. You cannot Chow honors, and you cannot Chow from across or from your right. After you Chow, you expose the sequence and discard one tile.

### Teacher-led emphasis
